 #### Minds do not think in terms of absolutes
 
 The title of this subsection represents one of the so-called "annoying features of the mind". What it says is that our minds don't think in absolute terms but always think in comparative reference points. While you might find yourself thinking "yeah, I knew that", truly internalizing what this means is really powerful.
 
 Let's talk about your GPA in school. If you're in a place where everyone is smart, it will not be straightforward for you to obtain even an average GPA (unless you're a genius who knows the material from elsewhere, of course). Even if you're putting a tremendous amount of effort and actually learning a lot
  your GPA might still be okay-ish but not that impressive. So there you are, suffering the impostor syndrome and feeling like you're trash. Meanwhile, your friend who ended up in a more average college is breezing through all assignments
   and exams and getting that perfect GPA with much less effort and actual knowledge (this is not necessarily the case, but it is an illustrative example). Most importantly, they feel better about themselves (which can motivate them to pursue more challenging opportunities). Hopefully, you see the point; if only minds did not have this annoying feature of thinking about everything in relative terms, maybe we'd be happy with what we have, knowing that in absolute terms we are doing great by just being in college and having the opportunity to be educated.
   
   Now, one must admit that this annoying feature is there for a reason. The absolute line of thinking is ambiguous. What is the lowest you can be, i.e. where is the rock-bottom? Being dead? One can argue that not being born is worse than that. But then, anyone you know is better than that rock-bottom and it is not too meaningful. Same thing for the highest point you can be, if there is such a limit. The point is, it is beneficial to
   think in relative terms to some extent. It tells you how good you're doing in certain aspects compared to others (though often detrimental to your happiness and satisfaction) and it makes you identify where you could improve. After all, society often works in relative ways. Colleges often look at your ranking in school, recruiters compare you to your peer applicants, your parents compare you to your siblings or friends in certain aspects. 
   Maybe the best approach would be to account for differences in resources and
   circumstances when comparing these individuals and try to come up with a ranking on the absolute scale.
   
   An alternative way of thinking is to stop comparing yourself with others too much, or at least try to remind yourself of this annoying feature
   and try to avoid the negative feeling that often comes with it. As cheesy as it sounds, try to be the best version of yourself and be better than you were yesterday. Keep in mind how far you can go by being aware of the best ones in your area of interest, but
   use them only as a reference to get a sense of how you're doing and understand that it doesn't mean you're not good enough, only that you can be that much (or potentially more if you're ambitious) better than you already are. And knowing this is a good thing.
   
   
   
   ### Hedonic adaptation - getting used to the positive and negative things that are repetitive
   
   > “Among life's cruelest truths is this one: wonderful things are especially wonderful the first time they happen, but their wonderfulness wanes with repetition.” - Daniel Todd Gilbert

Think of something wonderful that happened to you in your life. For me, that was getting into MIT.  Waking up the first morning after I realized I got admitted was one of the best feelings maybe ever. Now, 4 years after, I don't wake up every morning thinking "OMG I go to MIT wow I am so happy". Nope. Not at all. I have 
hedonically adapted to that fact and it does not excite me anymore. Hopefully, you don't judge me for that and realize that you would have done the same in my place. And this is normal, but also a bit sad because it means that anything super positive in your life will be short-lasted and be replaced with new problems and blessings over time. On
the positive note, even the bad things that hurt you so much will also wean off. Life will go on regardless, the sun will rise again.
 > Good or bad, that too shall pass

While again, this is not something unknown to you, being explicitly aware of hedonic adaptation when making decisions or reacting to outcomes can be life-changing.
Say you're choosing a college to go to. It is indeed an important decision that will affect a lot of things down the line. Let's say you're admitted to MIT and college X, where college X is not that well-known and doesn't have the same hype as MIT. 
While going to MIT will surely grant you a boost of serotonin the first couple of weeks/months, you will get used to it and the things that will remain are the things you wish you considered more thoroughly. Once the honeymoon phase is done, you will have to live with your choice and all the good and bad things that come with it. 
A wise move would be to truly try to understand the pros and cons of each choice, really thinking a couple of steps ahead, while taking into consideration YOUR desires, circumstances, beliefs and capabilities.
Ideally, once you have made your decision, try to make the best out of it and try to stop secretly wishing you did something else, thinking that the other choice would be much better (while this might objectively be the case, it is pointless to desire for things provided you cannot really change them at the time).

A similar reasoning holds for negative outcomes; you might think that losing your job/internship is the end of the world, but chances are you will move on. Sure enough, you will be hurt for a while, and it will
likely affect other areas in your life (maybe your relationships with other people), but chances are it will not be the "end of the world". 
Knowing this explicitly might help you let go of some things that don't serve you in your life; while abandoning them might hurt you in the present, you will end up better off in the long term. The reason why it *seems* to us that it will be the end of the world brings me to the next point I want to discuss:



### Focalism - when we think about the event, we only focus on that event, but not everything else

When you think about losing your job or getting a low grade or breaking up with your significant other, you are really ONLY thinking about that and tightly related events, rarely about any other good things that do remain even after those "tragic events". Like, no matter which of the three things happen to you (you can add more stuff to the list), the sun will still rise,
your favorite coffee shop will still work (unless it's a pandemic :) ), your family and friends will mostly be around, you can still go travel to a city where nobody knows you and you don't have to justify anything to anybody, you can still play your favorite games, watch movies, eat your favorite dishes, pick up a new hobby. Life has many aspects and letting ONE thing (no matter how major it seems) affect your
perception of yourself is simply wrong. Nonetheless, that is one more annoying aspect of the brain; this is just how we are and it makes us unhappy. 
Overcoming this is not easy when it is so built-in in our own minds, but one can help it by first being explicitly aware of its existence and then
acting in accordance with that knowledge.

## Conclusion

This post is getting long and there is so much more I could write. If you don't have the chance to take the course, try to refer to the summary I linked to in the beginning. There are sections about better wanting (what should we really want in life), about signature strengths (that can help you
find a job you actually like), about flow (immersive activities that make you truly in the moment, when "time ceases to exist"), having a growth mindset (believing that you can get better with practice and hard work) and all other good stuff. 

Understanding our brain, its functioning, our behaviors, our intercations with others and how it all affects us is a science in itself and I admire those who study it. This course is provides a glimpse into these sciences and
I am fascinated by how much of what we experience every day it can explain. I am thinking, how much better would one be as a human if they knew all these things and actually implement them in their daily lives? There is a real value in these theories, if we only take the time to explore them.
