#### It's not just a physical waiting line
In a nutshell, queues are pretty much everywhere and there's more science to it that you might think! It's not just the physical queues at the airports and Chipotle that we're talking about;
there are life-and-death queues (people waiting for organ donors), queues in computer networking (packets waiting to be transmitted), queues in nature (ants, birds) and some obscure ones like stampedes or airshows.

The places where there is a lot of waiting involved are very interesting to analyze. Think of airports. It's not a 
coincidence that you usually have to walk quite a lot from your gate to the baggage pick-up area. It's also not a coincidence that
the most common queue structure you see is one big serpentine line and multiple servers. These are all techniques to make the traveler a little bit less bored and more satisfied.


#### Psychology of waiting lines

 When it comes to customer satisfaction what truly matters is how the customer feels about the wait as opposed to how the wait really is. Maister (1985) investigated the psychology of waiting lines and gave eight propositions:

   - Unoccupied time feels longer than occupied time 
   - Pre-process waits feel longer than in-process waits 
   - Anxiety makes waits feel longer 
   - Uncertain waits seem longer than known, finite waits 
   - Unexplained waits seem longer than explained waits 
   - Unfair waits seem longer than equitable waits 
   - The more valuable the service, the longer people will wait 
   - Solo waiting feels longer than group waiting
