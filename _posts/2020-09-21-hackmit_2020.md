After the [last year's HackMIT victory](https://sanjass.github.io/notes/2019/09/19/hack_mit)
 of me and my team Minerva,
 I decided to take part in this year's virtual [HackMIT 2020 hackathon](https://hackmit.org/#cover) as well. 
 Except that this year I joined
 as a mentor and judge instead! In a similar fashion like in the post describing 
 my experience as a hacker, I want
 to give you a glimpse how it is to be on the other side. While this year's experience was 
  different than any other due to the virtual nature of the event, 
 I can attest that the fundamental spirit of the hackathon was preserved and the hackathon was
 a huge success. Speaking of that, I want to thank all of the organizers who probably got very little sleep
 those 2-3 days successfully making this virtual hackathon to run as seamlessly as possible.

 ## Mentors
 This year there were at least two types of mentors: general mentors - who monitor the queue at certain times, and
 beginner mentors, who are assigned to beginner teams to assist the team throughout the hackathon.
 As I signed up for both I will share my experience for both.
 ### Help queue mentor
 A hacker can request help online in the so called help-queue where they submit a ticket with brief info about their
 question and wait for a mentor to claim their ticket. In the beginning hackers usually want advice on approaching
 project ideas, need help setting up their environment or want to know what tools are best for their project.
 Later on, they tend to ask more and more specific questions about errors they are getting, ways to add specific
 functionalities in their project or how to use certain sponsor's APIs and products.
 Towards the end it becomes harder and harder to resolve certain tickets as the questions are way too specific and
 require deep familiarity with a particular often obscure technology. In general, a lot of people seemed to be
 interested in using Flask (Python web dev framework), Firebase (realtime database) and web dev tools including but not
 limited to React, Node, Javascript, HTML, CSS, databases (MongoDB, MySQL) etc.
 
 ### Beginner mentors
 These are mentors assigned to teams who qualify as beginner teams and they serve to assist and guide the team
 throughout the whole hackathon. While this might sound like a huge time-commitment it's actually not, as hackers
 are instructed that the beginner mentors serve mostly for general guidance and will be available sporadically.
  They can help in things like ensuring the beginner teams know what they need to do, that they should meet the deadlines,
  how to pitch their project and similar. This is put in place so that beginner hackers have some sort of support system
  in a time where the virtualization might make the experience isolating, especially for beginners.
  
  
 ## Judges
 Judges can sign up to judge 6 or 12 projects. Each project team presents virtually to the present judges for 4 minutes and
 they leave 4 minutes for Q&A. The judges then have 2 minutes offline to prepare for the next judging. I can say
 that it all happened so quickly. Time flies when you have 6 or 12 projects back to back.
 If a team is unable to present to a judge for whatever reason, they are not explicitly penalized but they are not
 included in the ranking the judge submits. While I am not sure how the unranked projects were handled
 I hope they got their chance to showcase their ideas.
 
 ## My experience
 I signed up as a mentor to a beginner team and I kept in touch with them throughout the hackathon.
 I shared my best tips and tricks about how to make most of the time, what to focus on and how to
 present the ideas. I found their idea to be surprisingly insightful and I was pleasantly surprised when
 I heard they won one of the sponsor's prizes! On the other hand, I also helped a couple of hackers on the
  mentor queue. After trying to debug virtually via Zoom I appreciate in-person collaboration much more now :).
  When it comes to judging I was pleasantly surprised of everything the teams achieved, starting from their
  ideas to the complexity of their design and implementation. If it was up to me, I would give all of them a 
  huge prize for working collaboratively and passionately to pull their ideas off until the end. 
  
  
 ## Conclusion
 I am happy to have had the experience to be not only a hacker but also a mentor and judge at HackMIT.
 The only thing I am missing now is being part of the organizing team. While I believe I won't qualify 
 for that next year due to graduation, I hope to stay connected in different ways with the HackMIT community in the future.
